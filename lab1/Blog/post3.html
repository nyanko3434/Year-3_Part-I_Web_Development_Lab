The Power of Gratitude

By Nayan Khusu | 5 min read

If someone had told me a year ago that writing down three things I'm grateful for each day would significantly change my life, I probably would have been skeptical. It sounds too simple, too cliché. But here I am, a year later, absolutely convinced of the transformative power of gratitude.

The Beginning of My Gratitude Practice

I started my gratitude practice during a particularly stressful period. Deadlines were piling up, I was struggling with some challenging coursework, and I felt overwhelmed by everything on my plate. A friend suggested I try keeping a gratitude journal, and honestly, I only agreed because I was desperate to try anything that might help.

The first few days were awkward. I'd sit with my notebook, trying to think of things to be grateful for, and my mind would immediately jump to all the things that were going wrong. But I forced myself to write three things every night before bed, even if they seemed trivial.

The Shift in Perspective

Something interesting started happening after about two weeks. I found myself actively looking for things to be grateful for throughout the day. During a tough lecture, instead of just feeling frustrated, I'd think, "But I'm grateful I have the opportunity to learn this." When a project didn't go as planned, I'd consider what I learned from the experience.

This wasn't toxic positivity – I wasn't ignoring or minimizing real challenges. Instead, I was training my brain to notice the good alongside the difficult. And that made all the difference.

The Ripple Effects

The benefits of this practice extended far beyond what I expected. My relationships improved because I started expressing more appreciation to the people around me. My stress levels decreased because I was focusing on what I had rather than what I lacked. Even my sleep improved because I was ending each day on a positive note.

I became more resilient too. When faced with setbacks, I found it easier to maintain perspective. Yes, this project failed, but I'm grateful for what I learned. Yes, this rejection stings, but I'm grateful for the opportunity to have tried.

Gratitude in Action

Here's what my gratitude practice looks like now:

Morning Gratitude: I start my day by thinking of three things I'm looking forward to. This sets a positive tone and helps me approach the day with enthusiasm.

Throughout the Day: I try to express gratitude to at least one person daily. A genuine thank you to a classmate who helped me understand a concept, a message to a friend who made me laugh, or appreciation for my family's support.

Evening Reflection: Before bed, I write down three specific things I'm grateful for from that day. The key is being specific – instead of "I'm grateful for my friends," I write "I'm grateful that Sarah took time to explain that algorithm to me when I was stuck."

The Science Behind It

I did some research to understand why this practice was so effective. It turns out there's solid science backing gratitude. Studies show that regularly practicing gratitude can improve mental health, strengthen relationships, enhance sleep quality, and even boost physical health.

The reason is relatively simple: our brains have a negativity bias – we're wired to notice and remember negative experiences more than positive ones. Gratitude practice counteracts this by intentionally directing our attention to the positive, effectively rewiring our neural pathways over time.

Overcoming Obstacles

I won't pretend it's always easy. There are days when I'm so tired or stressed that the last thing I want to do is think about what I'm grateful for. On really tough days, my gratitude list might be as basic as "I'm grateful for my bed" or "I'm grateful for coffee." And that's okay. The practice isn't about being profound – it's about being consistent.

An Invitation

If you're reading this and feeling skeptical like I was, I challenge you to try it for just one week. Keep a simple gratitude journal. Write down three things each day. They don't have to be big or impressive. They just have to be true.

Notice what happens. Notice how it affects your mood, your interactions with others, your overall outlook on life. You might be surprised, as I was, by how such a simple practice can create such profound change.

Gratitude hasn't eliminated challenges from my life, but it has changed how I experience them. It's shifted my focus from what's missing to what's present, from what's wrong to what's right. And in doing so, it's made my life immeasurably richer.

What are you grateful for today?
